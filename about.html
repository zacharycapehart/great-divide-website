Our Story

About The Great Divide

743 E. New York Street sits at the edge of two of Indianapolis’s oldest neighborhoods. Lockerbie Square is to the north — brick sidewalks, gas lamps, the kind of quiet that feels earned. Holy Cross is to the south — working-class roots, front porches, neighbors who have lived on the same block for decades. The Great Divide sits between them. The name is geographic, but what happens inside is the opposite of division. This is where the two neighborhoods meet.

Walk in on a Tuesday and you will recognize the same faces as Friday. The bartender knows what you drink. The regulars know each other by name. Conversations start easily and go longer than expected. Nobody is performing. Nobody is trying to impress anyone. The Great Divide is the kind of place that becomes part of your routine without you deciding it — you just keep coming back, and one day you realize you’re a regular too.

The menu is pub food, done properly. Burgers built the way they should be. Fish and chips with real beer batter. A shepherd’s pie that tastes like someone’s grandmother made it. Soup that changes with the week. Nothing on the menu will surprise you, and that is entirely the point. This is the food people actually want to eat — familiar, satisfying, made with care and served without ceremony.

The bar is cold beer, good whiskey, wine by the glass, and soft drinks for the non-drinkers. Straightforward and well-stocked. The bartenders are attentive without hovering. The bar at The Great Divide is a place to sit and stay, not a place to be rushed through.

Most evenings, there is music. A piano player on a Tuesday. A local duo on Friday. The music fills the space without taking it over — you can still hear the person across the table, which is how it should be in a pub. The performers are local, the sets are relaxed, and nobody is charging a cover.

If you live in Lockerbie Square or Holy Cross, this is your pub. If you are visiting Indianapolis and want to see the city the way people who live here see it, skip the downtown tourist spots and find your way to New York Street. Come in, sit down, and order a beer. Within twenty minutes you will understand why people come back.

The Neighborhood

Lockerbie Square to the north, Holy Cross to the south. The Great Divide sits between them — and has for years.

The Atmosphere

Warm, unpretentious, and social. The room is comfortable in the way that only comes with time. Nothing here was designed to look lived-in. It simply is.

The Regulars

We have plenty of them. They know each other by name, and they will probably say hello when you come in.
